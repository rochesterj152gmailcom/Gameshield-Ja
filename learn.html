Topic 1: Phishing

Spot the Scam Before You Click

Phishing is the most familiar threat among Jamaican gamers (65% of survey respondents knew it). Attackers disguise fake pages and messages as legitimate gaming platforms, prize notifications, or support requests. In gaming communities, this often comes through DMs, Discord servers, and in-game chats.

⚠ Fake urgency

"Act now or your account gets deleted" is a pressure tactic designed to make you panic and skip verification.

⚠ Suspicious sender addresses

Check the full domain, not just the display name. "epic-prizes-bonus.net" is not Epic Games.

⚠ Too-good-to-be-true offers

Free V-Bucks, free skins, or rare item giveaways are almost always designed to steal your credentials.

Topic 2: Passwords and MFA

One Breach Shouldn't Unlock Everything

Our survey found 71.6% of Jamaican gamers reuse passwords. When one platform gets breached, attackers try those same credentials on your email, other games, and payment accounts. Multi-factor authentication (MFA) acts as a second lock even if your password is stolen, they can't get in without the second factor.

Use unique passwords per account

One breach should not cascade into every account you own. A password manager makes this easy to manage.

Turn on MFA everywhere

Use an authenticator app, SMS code, or approval prompt. Even 50% of surveyed gamers already use this join them.

Secure your email first

Your email is the recovery point for all your gaming accounts. If attackers get your email, they can reset everything else.

Topic 3: Safe Browsing

Think Before You Click

37.9% of surveyed gamers never read app privacy policies or permissions before installing. Here's what to watch for.

🌐

Check URLs carefully

Look for spelling tricks (st3am.com), fake domains, or unusual endings before entering any login details or payment info.

⬇

Only download from official sources

Stick to the Google Play Store, App Store, Steam, and verified publisher sites. Unofficial mods often carry hidden malware.

📢

Ignore fake urgency

Messages that pressure you to act within minutes are designed to stop you thinking clearly. Real rewards don't expire in 10 minutes.

🔒

Read app permissions

If a game requests access to your contacts, microphone, or location for no clear reason, that's a red flag worth investigating before installing.

👥

Don't share account details

41.7% of surveyed gamers have shared credentials with friends or family. This creates accountability and recovery problems if something goes wrong.

🔄

Keep apps updated

Security patches fix known vulnerabilities. Delaying updates common among Jamaican mobile gamers per our research leaves known weaknesses open.

Topic 4: Social Engineering & Account Safety

Attackers Don't Always Need to Hack You

Our survey found 41.7% of Jamaican gamers have shared their account credentials with someone — and 25.2% have had their account hacked. Attackers exploit the trust built inside gaming communities. In-game chats, Discord DMs, and fake "support" messages let them manipulate you into handing over access willingly. This is social engineering: manipulation, not malware.

Researchers specifically note that Jamaica's youth gaming demographic — predominantly aged 18–24 — exhibit notable deficiencies in recognizing these trust-based attacks.

⚠ Fake "game support" in DMs

Legitimate platforms will never ask for your password through in-game chat, Discord, or WhatsApp. Any "support agent" who does is an attacker.

⚠ Never share credentials — even with friends

Account sharing creates security and recovery problems. If the friendship changes or their device is compromised, your account is at risk too.

Use a password manager

Our research explicitly recommends adopting a password manager (e.g. Bitwarden, Google Password Manager). One breach stops there instead of cascading across every account you own.
